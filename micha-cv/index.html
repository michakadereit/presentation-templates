Chapter I

Where it began

It ran. Flawlessly.

For nearly a decade I built Java backends that simply worked — Spring Boot services, ELK observability, Docker Swarm, NATS. From insurance products at Faktor Zehn to secure messaging at Zertificon, where I led a chapter. Scalable, maintainable, resilient. I had mastered the craft — and that was the exact moment the ground began to move.

scroll
Chapter II

The shift

Then the ground moved.

Almost overnight, the rules of software changed. AI could write code, reason about systems, generate tests. The hand-crafted certainty that takes years to earn suddenly felt like it might not be enough. For a seasoned engineer, that is a quiet, unsettling kind of fall — not a crash, but the slow realization that yesterday's mastery guarantees nothing tomorrow.

Chapter III

The low point

„February 2024. First week at CGM ONE. A new domain — telemedicine, video consultations. A new stack — OpenSearch, Filebeat, Ansible. And one open question no framework could answer: will these AI tools replace me, or multiply me?“

— the question I couldn't outsource

Chapter IV

The turn

One decision changed the course.

Instead of resisting the wave, I leaned all the way in. I put Claude Code, GitHub Copilot and Devin to work in my daily flow — not as toys, but as force multipliers. And I doubled down on the fundamentals that make AI safe to trust: Clean Architecture, Domain-Driven Design, Test-Driven and Spec-Driven Development with Playwright. The insight: AI doesn't replace great engineers. It multiplies them.

Chapter V

The climb

And suddenly, it took off.

I became an AI Champion at CGM — then a coach, helping engineers adopt these tools with judgment. I started presenting, not only to my own team but to others. I led two AI hackathons: one for my team, one for another. The craft hadn't been replaced. It had found a new gear.
